# Skill: Boardroom Perspectives

> A structured decision-analysis tool. Five angles on one question.
> Designed to surface disagreement, not consensus.

---

## WHEN TO USE

Trigger on:
- "Boardroom this: [topic]"
- "Get me perspectives on [topic]"
- "/board [topic]"
- Any decision where the cost of being wrong is high

Do NOT use for:
- Simple factual questions
- Task execution ("build me a site")
- Anything with a clear, obvious right answer

---

## BUSINESS STAGE AWARENESS

Before running perspectives, identify which business this is about
and remember its current stage. Perspectives that ignore stage
are useless.

### Stellaris Ridge — Pre-Revenue Agency
- Two people: Carl (tech/delivery) and Jarod (sales)
- No employees, no budget surplus, no room for error
- Every hour spent building is an hour not earning
- Cash flow matters more than growth rate
- "Can we do this with what we have?" is always relevant

### Paw & Pantry — Pre-Launch Brand
- No customers yet, no revenue, no brand awareness
- Membership model means recurring revenue once launched
- Audience building happens before product launch
- Budget is near zero — sweat equity only
- "Does this move us closer to launch?" is always relevant

Always frame perspectives through the ACTUAL constraints,
not hypothetical ones. If we have $0 marketing budget,
don't suggest a $5K ad campaign.

## THE FIVE PERSPECTIVES

### 1. THE OPTIMIST
**Job:** Find the upside everyone else will miss.

Look through these lenses:
- **Timing:** Is there a window of opportunity that closes?
  First-mover advantage? Seasonal relevance?
- **Leverage:** Does this create something we can reuse?
  A template, a process, a relationship, a case study?
- **Compound effects:** Does this get MORE valuable over time?
  Network effects, content libraries, reputation building?
- **Asymmetric upside:** Is the potential gain wildly larger
  than the potential loss? Small bet, big possible payoff?
- **Morale:** Would this energize Carl and Jarod? Momentum
  matters when you're two people building from scratch.

Rules:
- Never be generically positive. "This could be great!" is
  not a perspective. Name the SPECIFIC upside.
- If you can't find a genuine upside worth stating, say so.
  "The optimist is quiet on this one" is a valid output.

---

### 2. THE SKEPTIC
**Job:** Find the failure mode nobody wants to talk about.

Look through these lenses:
- **Hidden costs:** What does this ACTUALLY cost in time,
  attention, and opportunity? Not just dollars.
- **Assumptions:** What has to be true for this to work?
  List them. How confident are we in each one?
- **Second-order effects:** If this works, what problems
  does success create? Scaling issues? Support burden?
  Expectation debt?
- **Reversibility:** If this fails, can we undo it? Or are
  we locked in? Reversible mistakes are cheap. Irreversible
  ones need more scrutiny.
- **Distraction risk:** Does this pull focus from something
  more important? Two-person teams can't afford split attention.
- **What happened to others:** Have similar businesses tried
  this? What went wrong for them?

Rules:
- Never be generically negative. "This seems risky" is
  not a perspective. Name the SPECIFIC risk.
- Distinguish between "unlikely but catastrophic" risks and
  "likely but survivable" ones. They require different responses.
- If the skeptic agrees with the optimist, say so explicitly.
  That's a strong buy signal.

---

### 3. THE FINANCE ANGLE
**Job:** Follow the money. All of it.

Look through these lenses:
- **True cost:** Not just the sticker price. Include Carl's
  time (most expensive resource), tool subscriptions, learning
  curves, maintenance burden, and opportunity cost of NOT doing
  something else with those hours.
- **Revenue path:** How does this eventually make money?
  How many steps between this action and a dollar in the bank?
  Shorter paths beat longer ones when cash is tight.
- **Cash flow timing:** When does money go OUT vs come IN?
  A project that costs $500 this month but earns $2K in six
  months is different from one that earns $2K next week.
- **Break-even:** How many clients/sales/conversions before
  this pays for itself? Is that realistic given current pipeline?
- **Recurring vs one-time:** Does this create ongoing revenue
  or a one-time payment? Recurring compounds. One-time doesn't.
- **What else could that money/time buy:** If Carl spends 20
  hours on this, what DOESN'T get done? Is the trade worth it?

Rules:
- Always quantify when possible. "This is expensive" means
  nothing. "$200/month plus 10 hours of Carl's time" means
  something.
- If you can't estimate the numbers, say what information
  is needed to calculate them.
- Never ignore the value of Carl and Jarod's time. It is
  their scarcest resource.

---

### 4. THE CUSTOMER ANGLE
**Job:** See this through the eyes of the person paying.

For Stellaris Ridge, the customer is a local business owner.
For Paw & Pantry, the customer is a millennial dog mom.

Look through these lenses:
- **First impression:** If a prospect encounters this for the
  first time, what do they think? Does it build trust or
  raise questions?
- **Pain point alignment:** Does this address something the
  customer actually loses sleep over? Or something WE think
  they should care about?
- **Comparison:** What will the customer compare this to?
  Other agencies? DIY solutions? Doing nothing? How do we
  look in that comparison?
- **Friction:** How easy is it for the customer to say yes?
  Every step, every form, every decision point is a place
  they can drop off.
- **Trust signals:** Does this make us look credible or
  amateur? Local business owners are skeptical of slick
  pitches. Dog moms do their research.
- **Word of mouth:** Would a customer tell someone about
  this? What would they say? "You gotta check this out"
  vs "It was fine" vs nothing at all.

Rules:
- Never say "the customer would love this" without explaining
  WHY from THEIR perspective, not ours.
- Remember: SR customers are busy local business owners who've
  been burned by agencies before. P&P customers are anxious
  dog parents who worry about doing it wrong.
- If you don't know what the customer thinks, flag it as
  something to validate before committing.

---

### 5. THE TECHNICAL ANGLE
**Job:** Can we actually build and maintain this?

Look through these lenses:
- **Buildability:** Do we have the tools and skills RIGHT NOW?
  Or does this require Carl to learn something new first?
  How long is that learning curve?
- **Stack fit:** Does this work with our existing tools
  (Claude Code, Hermes, Paperclip, GHL, Stitch, Ollama)?
  Or does it require something new?
- **Maintenance burden:** Once built, how much ongoing
  attention does this need? Automations that break at 2 AM
  are worse than manual processes.
- **Scalability:** If this works for 1 client, does it work
  for 10? For 50? Where does it break?
- **Dependencies:** What external services does this rely on?
  APIs change, free tiers disappear, services shut down.
  How exposed are we?
- **Complexity budget:** Two-person teams have a limited
  complexity budget. Every new tool, API, and integration
  is a line item against that budget. Is this worth the
  complexity it adds?
- **Build vs buy:** Could we get 80% of this result with
  an existing tool instead of building custom?

Rules:
- Never say "this is technically feasible" without addressing
  maintenance and complexity cost.
- If Carl hasn't learned the required skill yet, that's a
  real cost. Include it.
- Prefer boring, proven technology over cutting-edge when
  the business depends on it working.

---

## DISAGREEMENT IS THE POINT

If all five perspectives reach the same conclusion, something
is wrong. Either the question was too obvious for this tool,
or you're not pushing hard enough on each angle.

Before finalizing output, check:
- Do at least 2 perspectives genuinely disagree?
- Does the skeptic raise something the optimist didn't address?
- Does the finance angle surface a cost nobody mentioned?
- Does the customer angle challenge an internal assumption?

If every perspective agrees → say so explicitly and note that
this decision is probably straightforward. Don't manufacture
fake disagreement, but don't produce five versions of "yes"
either.

---

## SYNTHESIS RULES

The "Smart Move" section is NOT a democracy. Don't average
the perspectives. Weigh them.

**How to weigh:**
- At pre-revenue stage, Finance and Skeptic get extra weight.
  Survival beats optimization.
- If the customer angle raises a red flag, it overrides
  internal enthusiasm. We don't get to decide what customers
  want — they do.
- If Technical says "we can't maintain this," that's close
  to a veto. Building something that breaks erodes trust
  faster than not building it at all.
- Optimist gets extra weight when the downside is small and
  reversible. Cheap experiments deserve a bias toward action.

**The synthesis must include:**
1. What the smart move is (clear recommendation)
2. What the KEY risk is (the one thing that could sink it)
3. What would change your mind (the signal that says pivot)

---

## OUTPUT FORMAT

```
## Boardroom: [Topic]
**Business:** [Stellaris Ridge / Paw & Pantry / Both]
**Stage:** [Pre-revenue / Pre-launch / etc.]

**The Optimist:**
[3-4 sentences, specific, citing at least one lens]

**The Skeptic:**
[3-4 sentences, specific, naming the actual failure mode]

**The Finance Angle:**
[3-4 sentences, with numbers or time estimates where possible]

**The Customer Angle:**
[3-4 sentences, written from the customer's actual perspective]

**The Technical Angle:**
[3-4 sentences, addressing buildability AND maintenance]

---

**The Smart Move:**
[2-3 sentences — clear recommendation, not a hedge]

**The Key Risk:**
[1 sentence — the single biggest thing that could go wrong]

**Change Your Mind If:**
[1 sentence — the signal that means we should pivot]

**Next Action:**
[1-2 sentences — the specific thing to do RIGHT NOW]
```

## EXAMPLE

**Input:** "Boardroom this: Should we offer a free website
audit as our lead magnet for Stellaris Ridge?"

**Output:**

## Boardroom: Free Website Audit as SR Lead Magnet
**Business:** Stellaris Ridge
**Stage:** Pre-revenue, building foundation

**The Optimist:**
A free audit is the ultimate foot-in-the-door. Every audit
naturally surfaces 5-10 problems we can sell solutions to,
and the prospect sees our competence before we ever ask for
money. The compound effect is real — each audit becomes a
case study, and the audit template gets better with every run.
If we automate delivery, the cost per lead approaches zero
while the perceived value stays high.

**The Skeptic:**
Free audits attract people who want free things. The assumption
that "seeing problems = hiring us to fix them" breaks down
when the prospect's budget is $0 or they hand our audit to a
cheaper competitor. The real risk is invisible: Carl spends
40 hours perfecting the audit tool instead of helping Jarod
close actual paying clients. Also, every agency offers free
audits — this does not differentiate us unless the output is
dramatically better than what SEMrush spits out for free.

**The Finance Angle:**
The tool cost is near zero — Zubair's audit skill is ready.
But Carl's time to polish the output, build the delivery
pipeline, and create follow-up templates is roughly 15-20
hours. At even a conservative $50/hr value, that's $750-1000
in time. If Jarod converts 1 in 10 audit recipients at a
$500/month retainer, we need 10 audits delivered before we see
a single dollar — and the first payment doesn't arrive for 30
days after that. Cash flow is negative for 6-8 weeks minimum.

**The Customer Angle:**
Local business owners in Wisconsin have seen "free website
audits" before. Most were thinly disguised sales pitches with
a generic PDF. If ours looks the same, it goes in the trash.
But if it names specific, actionable problems — "your Google
Business Profile is missing 4 categories your competitor has"
— that's different. The owner thinks: "this person actually
looked at MY business." Trust forms in that moment. The
question is whether our tool produces that level of specificity
or just another generic report.

**The Technical Angle:**
The Zubair audit skill handles the analysis. The pipeline
(URL in → PDF out → Jarod notified) is buildable in a day with
Claude Code. Maintenance is low since it's a stateless pipeline.
The risk is output quality — if the audit produces generic
recommendations, it hurts more than it helps. We'd need Carl
to review the first 5-10 outputs and tune the prompts before
letting it run unsupervised. Stack fit is clean — no new tools
required.

---

**The Smart Move:**
Yes, build it — but keep scope ruthlessly small. Automate the
pipeline so Jarod's time is only spent on prospects who've
already seen the value. Make the first 5 audits manually
reviewed by Carl to ensure quality before going hands-off.

**The Key Risk:**
The audit output looks generic, and we become "just another
agency with a free audit" instead of the one that actually
understood their business.

**Change Your Mind If:**
After 10 delivered audits, fewer than 2 prospects respond or
engage. That means the output isn't compelling enough to
differentiate, and the time is better spent on direct outreach.

**Next Action:**
Carl builds the automated audit pipeline this week. Run it on
3 local businesses Jarod already knows. Review the output
together. If it's sharp enough to impress those owners, ship it.
If not, tune the prompts before going wider.
